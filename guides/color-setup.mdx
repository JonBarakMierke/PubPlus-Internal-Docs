Color is one of the most common sources of surprise when a finished print job comes off the press. What looked vivid and bold on your monitor can appear duller or shifted in the physical piece — not because something went wrong, but because screens and printers speak fundamentally different color languages. Understanding those differences and preparing your files in the right color mode puts you in control of the final result before a single sheet goes to press.

## CMYK vs. RGB: Know the Difference

Every color in your file is defined in a color model. The two most common are CMYK and RGB, and they are **not** interchangeable for print.

| | CMYK | RGB |
|---|---|---|
| **Stands for** | Cyan, Magenta, Yellow, Black | Red, Green, Blue |
| **Used for** | Commercial and offset printing | Screens, monitors, web |
| **How it works** | Subtractive — inks absorb light | Additive — pixels emit light |
| **Color gamut** | Smaller range | Wider range |
| **Use when** | Preparing any file for print | Designing for digital display |

<Warning>
  Submitting an RGB file to a print shop does not mean your file prints in RGB. The press software converts it automatically — but automatic conversion is rarely accurate. Always convert to CMYK yourself so you control exactly how each color translates.
</Warning>

## Why Screen Colors Differ from Printed Output

Your monitor generates color by emitting red, green, and blue light — it can produce colors that physical inks simply cannot replicate, including many bright neons and highly saturated blues and greens. When those colors are converted to CMYK ink, they are mapped to the closest printable equivalent, which is often noticeably different.

As a general rule, expect **85–90% visual similarity** between what you see on a calibrated monitor and the finished print. Preparing your file in CMYK and previewing it in CMYK mode gives you the most accurate on-screen preview possible.

<Note>
  Monitor calibration affects how accurate your on-screen preview is. An uncalibrated monitor can make CMYK files look just as misleading as RGB files. If color fidelity is critical for your project, request a hard proof (see below).
</Note>

## Converting Your File to CMYK

<Steps>
  <Step title="Open your document's color settings">
    In Adobe Illustrator, go to **File → Document Color Mode → CMYK Color**. In Adobe InDesign, CMYK is the default for print documents — confirm it in **File → Document Setup**. In Photoshop, go to **Image → Mode → CMYK Color**.
  </Step>

  <Step title="Review color shifts after conversion">
    After switching modes, inspect every element in your file. Out-of-gamut RGB colors are remapped automatically — some may shift noticeably. Adjust individual swatches manually to your preferred CMYK values where needed.
  </Step>

  <Step title="Verify all swatches are CMYK">
    Open the Swatches or Color panel and confirm no swatch is labeled as RGB, HEX, or "Web Safe." Delete or replace any non-CMYK color definitions.
  </Step>

  <Step title="Check placed or embedded images">
    Images placed in your layout (photos, product shots) must also be in CMYK. Open each image in Photoshop and convert it to CMYK (**Image → Mode → CMYK Color**) before re-linking or re-embedding it in your layout.
  </Step>

  <Step title="Export as a CMYK PDF">
    When exporting your final PDF, confirm the color output option is set to **CMYK** or **Convert to Destination (CMYK)**. Do not leave the color space as "no conversion" if your file contains any RGB elements.
  </Step>
</Steps>

## Soft Proofing vs. Hard Proofing

There are two ways to verify color before committing to a full print run.

<Accordion title="Soft proofing (on-screen simulation)">
  Soft proofing simulates how your CMYK file will look when printed, using a color profile that represents the press or paper. In Adobe applications, enable it under **View → Proof Colors** and select a CMYK output profile. Soft proofing is free, instant, and useful for catching major color shifts early — but it is only as accurate as your monitor's calibration.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Hard proofing (physical sample)">
  A hard proof is a physical printout produced before your full run, used to verify color, layout, and finish on actual paper stock. Hard proofs are the most reliable way to confirm color accuracy and are especially important for brand-sensitive jobs, large quantities, or any project where color matching is critical.
</Accordion>

<Tip>
  If your project involves brand colors, product photography, or any situation where color accuracy is non-negotiable, request a **hard proof** from Publications Plus before approving your full print run. A small upfront investment in a proof can prevent costly reprints.
</Tip>

## Rich Black for Large Dark Areas

Printing a large black area — a dark background, a full-bleed banner, or a bold headline block — using only 100% K (pure black ink) produces a flat, slightly washed-out result. That's because a single ink layer at full density still allows some paper show-through, and the surface can look uneven at large sizes.

<Warning>
  Do **not** use 100% K alone for large solid black areas. It will appear noticeably thin and uneven on press — especially on coated stocks and wide-format media.
</Warning>

Instead, use a **Rich Black** — a blend of all four CMYK inks that produces a deeper, more saturated black. Publications Plus recommends two tested formulas:

### Rich Black Formulas

<Accordion title="Rich Black Mix 1 — Standard (recommended for most print jobs)">
  | Ink | Value |
  |-----|-------|
  | Cyan | 60% |
  | Magenta | 40% |
  | Yellow | 40% |
  | Black | 100% |

  Use this formula for flyers, brochures, postcards, and most standard offset and digital print jobs.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Rich Black Mix 2 — Deep (for large-format and high-coverage pieces)">
  | Ink | Value |
  |-----|-------|
  | Cyan | 75% |
  | Magenta | 68% |
  | Yellow | 67% |
  | Black | 90% |

  Use this deeper mix for wide-format prints, banners, and any piece with extensive dark background coverage where maximum density is needed.
</Accordion>

<Note>
  Reserve Rich Black for large background areas and oversized headlines. For small body text and fine details, use 100% K only — Rich Black at small sizes can cause slight ink registration issues that blur thin strokes and serifs.
</Note>
