When a printer cuts a finished piece to its final size, the blade never lands in exactly the same spot twice — small mechanical shifts are unavoidable. Bleed is the extra artwork you add beyond the intended trim edge so that these tiny variations never leave an unwanted white sliver on the finished product. Setting up bleed correctly before you export is one of the most important steps in preparing a print-ready file.

## What Is Bleed?

Bleed is an extension of your background colors, images, or patterns that reaches beyond the final trim size of your document. When the press sheet is cut down, the trim line falls somewhere inside that extended area, guaranteeing that ink or color runs all the way to the physical edge of the finished piece.

<Note>
  Publications Plus requires **0.25" (¼ inch) of bleed on all four sides** of your document. Files submitted without bleed may be returned for correction or printed with visible white edges.
</Note>

## Understanding the Three Zones

Think of your document as having three concentric rectangles:

- **Bleed area** — The outermost boundary, 0.25" beyond the trim on every side. Extend all background elements and full-bleed images to this edge.
- **Trim line** — The intended cut line. This is the final size of your finished piece (e.g., 8.5" × 11").
- **Safe zone** — An inner boundary at least 0.125" inside the trim line. Keep all critical content — text, logos, barcodes — inside this area so nothing important is accidentally cut off.

```text
┌─────────────────────────────────┐  ← Bleed edge (document export size)
│         BLEED AREA (0.25")      │
│  ┌───────────────────────────┐  │  ← Trim line (final finished size)
│  │      SAFE ZONE (0.125")   │  │
│  │  ┌─────────────────────┐  │  │  ← Safe zone inner boundary
│  │  │                     │  │  │
│  │  │   YOUR CONTENT HERE │  │  │
│  │  │  (text, logos, etc.)│  │  │
│  │  └─────────────────────┘  │  │
│  │                           │  │
│  └───────────────────────────┘  │
│                                 │
└─────────────────────────────────┘
```

<Warning>
  Never place important content — including text, logos, phone numbers, or barcodes — inside the bleed area or within 0.125" of the trim line. Anything in that zone risks being trimmed off during cutting.
</Warning>

## How to Add 0.25" Bleed

<Steps>
  <Step title="Set your document size to the final trim size">
    Create your document at the finished dimensions you need — for example, 8.5" × 11" for a standard letter flyer. Do **not** manually inflate the canvas at this stage; let bleed settings handle the extra space.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Enable bleed in your document settings">
    In your design application, locate the bleed or document setup panel and enter **0.25"** (or 18 pt) for all four sides — top, bottom, left, and right. Most professional tools (Adobe Illustrator, InDesign, Affinity Publisher) have a dedicated bleed field in the new-document dialog or in Document Setup.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Extend background elements to the bleed edge">
    Drag or resize any background images, color fills, or patterns so they reach the bleed boundary — the outer dashed or red guide line shown by your software. Do not stop at the trim line.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Keep critical content inside the safe zone">
    Move all text, logos, and key graphics so they sit at least 0.125" inside the trim line. Most applications let you add a second guide set at that offset to make this easy to verify visually.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Export at the correct size including bleed">
    When exporting your PDF, check the option to include bleed marks and set the bleed value to **0.25"**. The exported file dimensions will be larger than your trim size by 0.5" in each direction.

    **Example:** A final size of 8.5" × 11" exports as **9" × 11.5"**.
  </Step>
</Steps>

## Common Bleed Mistakes

<Accordion title="My background stops at the trim line — do I need to redo it?">
  Yes. If your background color or image ends at the trim line and the printer's cut drifts even slightly, a white gap will appear. Scale or reposition the element so it reaches the bleed edge (0.25" beyond trim on every side).
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Can I just make my document larger on each side instead of using bleed settings?">
  Avoid this approach. Building bleed into the document size rather than the bleed field confuses prepress software, shifts your safe-zone references, and may cause your file to be flagged during preflight. Always set bleed in the designated bleed field and keep the document size at the true trim dimensions.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="What if my design has a white background?">
  Even with a white background, extend it to the bleed edge. Press sheets are not pure white, and an untrimmed edge can reveal the substrate color. Filling the bleed area with white ensures a consistent result.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Does bleed apply to digital or screen output?">
  No. Bleed is only relevant for physical print production. If you are exporting for web, email, or screen display, export at your exact document size with no bleed.
</Accordion>
