## Trifold vs. Bifold

Choosing the right fold type shapes how your audience reads your content. Use the guide below to match your fold to your message.

<Tabs>
  <Tab title="Trifold">
    A trifold divides your sheet into **three equal panels** that fold in on each other — the most common brochure format in North America. The result is a compact, pocket-sized piece with six printable panel faces (three on each side).

    **Use a trifold when:**

    - You have a clear content hierarchy — cover, interior panels, and back panel each serve a distinct purpose
    - You want a format that fits neatly into standard brochure racks, display stands, and #10 envelopes
    - You're covering a product or service with multiple distinct sections (features, pricing, contact info)
    - You're distributing at trade shows, leaving at a reception desk, or mailing as a direct-mail piece

    **Design tip:** In a trifold layout, the rightmost panel of the outside face is your cover, and the leftmost panel is the back. The inside-right panel functions as a "reveal" panel that readers see first when they open the brochure — make it count.
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Bifold">
    A bifold divides your sheet in **half** with a single center fold, creating four panel faces (two on each side). The format feels more like a booklet or report cover and gives each panel more real estate than a trifold.

    **Use a bifold when:**

    - Your content benefits from larger images, charts, or maps that need room to breathe
    - You're producing a menu, program, or presentation folder insert where a clean, open spread matters
    - You want an upscale, editorial feel — bifolds tend to read as more premium than trifolds
    - Your messaging is concise and doesn't require six separate panels to tell the story

    **Design tip:** Because the inside of a bifold opens to a full two-panel spread, consider designing the interior as a single unified layout rather than two separate panels. This works especially well for dramatic photography or a bold headline moment.
  </Tab>
</Tabs>

## Choosing the Right Paper Stock

| Stock | Weight | Surface | Best For |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **60# Offset** | Light | Uncoated, matte | High-volume mail campaigns, writable surfaces |
| **70# Offset** | Medium | Uncoated, matte | General-purpose brochures, in-store handouts |
| **80# Gloss Text** | Medium-heavy | Coated, gloss | Retail and event promotions, color-rich designs |
| **100# Gloss Text** | Heavy | Coated, high gloss | Premium trade-show pieces, luxury brand materials |

<Tip>
  For **trade show displays and in-store racks**, choose **80# or 100# Gloss Text**. The heavier coated stock holds its shape in display stands, survives more handling without wrinkling, and makes photography and brand colors look their best under event lighting.
</Tip>

<Tip>
  For **direct-mail campaigns** where postage weight matters, **60# or 70# Offset** keeps piece weight down and reduces mailing costs — especially important when mailing large quantities.
</Tip>
