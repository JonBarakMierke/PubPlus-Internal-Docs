Submitting properly prepared artwork is the single most important step you can take to ensure your job prints exactly as you envisioned it. Our Pre-Press team reviews every file we receive, but catching and correcting problems before a job goes to press takes time and can delay your order. Follow the requirements on this page and your files will sail through Pre-Press and onto the press floor without a hitch. ## At a Glance: Quick Checklist

<Note>
  Before you send your files, confirm every item on this list:

  - ✅ **File format:** PDF (preferred), AI, or InDesign (.indd)
  - ✅ **Resolution:** 300 DPI minimum for all images and raster elements
  - ✅ **Color mode:** CMYK — not RGB, not Pantone-only
  - ✅ **Bleed:** 0.25″ (¼ inch) added to all four sides
  - ✅ **Fonts:** Embedded in the PDF, or all text converted to outlines
  - ✅ **Minimum font size:** 6–8pt for body text; 8pt minimum for wide-format
  - ✅ **Trim marks:** Include crop/trim marks set outside the bleed area
</Note>

## File Format

Save and submit your artwork as a **print-ready PDF** whenever possible. PDF is the industry-standard format for print because it packages your artwork, fonts, color data, and bleed information into a single portable file that renders consistently on any system and on press.

If you work in Adobe's creative suite, we also accept:

- **Adobe Illustrator (.ai)** — ensure all linked images are embedded and fonts are outlined before sending.
- **Adobe InDesign (.indd)** — use _Package_ (File → Package) to collect all linked files and fonts into a single folder, then compress and send the entire package.

<Warning>
  Do not submit artwork as JPEG, PNG, Word, or PowerPoint. These formats are not suitable for commercial printing — they lack bleed support, may downsample image data, and cannot reliably preserve color profiles. If this is all you have, contact our Design Department and we can help reformat or recreate your files.
</Warning>

## Resolution — 300 DPI Minimum

All raster images (photos, textures, and any element that isn't pure vector artwork) must be at least **300 DPI (dots per inch)** at the final print size. Images sourced from websites are typically 72–96 DPI and will appear blurry and pixelated when printed — even if they look sharp on screen.

<Tip>
  Check resolution in Photoshop via **Image → Image Size**. Make sure the resolution reads 300 DPI or higher at 100% of the intended print dimensions. Scaling a low-resolution image up in InDesign or Illustrator does not add pixels — it only stretches the existing ones.
</Tip>

Wide-format prints (banners, posters, large displays) are typically viewed from a greater distance, so a slightly lower effective resolution is acceptable — but 150 DPI at final size is the practical minimum for wide-format. When in doubt, submit the highest resolution available.

See the [Resolution & File Formats Guide](/guides/resolution-and-formats) for a deeper look at resolution, file compression, and acceptable image formats within your PDF.

## Color Mode — CMYK

Commercial printing uses four ink channels: **Cyan, Magenta, Yellow, and Black (CMYK)**. Submit all artwork in CMYK color mode. Do not submit files in RGB — RGB is an additive light-based model used for screens, and when it is converted to CMYK at press time the colors can shift significantly, especially bright greens, purples, and oranges.

Set your document to CMYK from the start in your design application:

- **Illustrator:** File → Document Color Mode → CMYK Color
- **InDesign:** File → New Document → Intent: Print (CMYK)
- **Photoshop:** Image → Mode → CMYK Color

<Warning>
  If your brand uses specific Pantone (PMS) spot colors, discuss this with our team before submitting. Our standard digital and offset presses print in process CMYK, not spot ink. Spot color simulations are possible but must be agreed upon in advance.
</Warning>

Visit the [Color Setup Guide](/guides/color-setup) for step-by-step instructions on converting RGB files to CMYK and managing color profiles correctly.

## Bleed — 0.25″ on All Sides

Bleed is the extra area of artwork that extends beyond the edge of your finished trim size. When a press sheet is cut to its final dimensions, the blade can vary by a small fraction — bleed ensures that no white paper border appears at the edge of your print if the cut lands slightly inside or outside the trim line.

**Extend all background colors, images, and design elements that touch the edge of your document by 0.25 inches (¼″) on every side.**

For example, if you're designing a 3.5″ × 2″ business card, your document canvas should be **4″ × 2.5″** — 0.25″ larger on all four sides.

Keep important content (text, logos, faces) at least **0.125″ inside the trim line** — this is called the _safe zone_ or _live area_. Content closer to the trim edge risks being cut off.

<Tip>
  Set up bleed in InDesign under **File → Document Setup → Bleed and Slug**. In Illustrator, configure bleed in the **Artboard Options** panel. When exporting your PDF, confirm that the bleed marks are included under **Marks and Bleeds** in the export dialog.
</Tip>

The [Bleed & Trim Guide](/guides/bleed-and-trim) walks through this setup visually with step-by-step screenshots for InDesign and Illustrator.

## Fonts — Embed or Outline

Every font used in your artwork must travel with your file so our systems render type exactly as you designed it. There are two ways to ensure this:

1. **Embed fonts when exporting your PDF.** In InDesign and Illustrator, the standard PDF/X-1a or PDF/X-4 export presets embed fonts automatically. Verify by opening your PDF export settings and confirming the "Embed All Fonts" or equivalent option is checked.
2. **Convert all text to outlines** before saving your Illustrator or InDesign file. Select all text (Edit → Select All), then **Type → Create Outlines**. This converts letters to vector shapes — no font file is needed, but the text becomes non-editable.

<Warning>
  If fonts are not embedded and the file arrives on a system that doesn't have those fonts installed, your text will either be substituted with a default font (changing your layout) or display as missing glyphs. Always embed or outline before submitting.
</Warning>

**Minimum font sizes:**

- Body and fine-print text: **6–8pt minimum**
- Wide-format / large-format prints: **8pt minimum**

Text smaller than these thresholds can become illegible in print even if it looks readable on screen. When sizing display text for wide-format, consider the viewing distance — larger is almost always better.

See the [Typography Guide](/guides/typography) for guidance on type sizing, tracking, and legibility across different print formats.
